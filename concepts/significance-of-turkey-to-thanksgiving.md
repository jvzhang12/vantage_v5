Turkey holds a prominent place at Thanksgiving for several reasons:

1. **Historical Roots:** Early accounts suggest that wildfowl, possibly including turkey, were served at the 1621 feast between Pilgrims and Wampanoag people. Being native to North America, turkeys were a locally sourced and practical choice for settlers.

2. **Practicality:** Turkeys are large and could feed many people, aligning with Thanksgiving's focus on communal gatherings. They became widely farmed and readily available.

3. **Cultural Symbol:** Over the 19th and 20th centuries, turkey became deeply culturally associated with Thanksgiving in popular media, recipes, and imagery (e.g., Norman Rockwell's 'Freedom from Want' painting). "Turkey Day" is a common nickname for the holiday.

4. **Traditions:** Eating turkey is now a widely observed tradition. Modern practices include the U.S. presidential turkey pardon, further cementing the bird's link to Thanksgiving.

While not all early Thanksgivings featured turkey, it has come to embody abundance and communal sharing—key aspects of the holiday spirit.
